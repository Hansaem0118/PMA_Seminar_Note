\begin{exercise}
Prove that a polynomial of degree $n$ is uniformly continuous on $\mathbb{R}$ if and
only if $n=0$ or $1$.
\end{exercise}
\begin{proof}
Let $f$ be a polynomial of degree $n$ and suppose that $n \geq 2$.
We wish to prove that for any $\delta >0$, there exist points $x,y \in \R$ such that $|x-y|<\delta$ and $|f(x)-f(y)|\geq 1$. 
Notice that for all $\delta>0$, there exists $M \in \R$ such that $x>M$ implies $|f'(x)|>2/\delta$, since $|f'(x)| \to \infty$ as $x \to \infty$.  
Let $x=M$ and $y=M+\delta/2$. 
By the Mean Value Theorem, choose $\xi \in (x,y)$ such that 
\[
f(x)-f(y)=f'(\xi)(x-y).
\]
Since $\xi>M$ and $|x-y|=\delta/2$, we have
\begin{align*}
    |f(x)-f(y)|&=|f'(\xi)||x-y|
    \\&>(2/\delta)\cdot|x-y|=1.
\end{align*}
Therefore, $f$ is not uniformly continuous on $\R$ if $n\geq 2$. 
Suppose that $n=1$. 
We write $f(x)=ax+b$ with $a\neq0$ and take $\delta=\varepsilon/|a|$ for every $\varepsilon>0$.
Then $|x-y|<\delta$ and $x,y \in \R$ imply $|f(x)-f(y)|=|a||x-y|<|a|\delta=\varepsilon$.
It is obvious that $f$ is uniformly continuous on $\R$ if $n=0$. We conclude that $f$ is uniformly continuous on $\R$ if and only if $n=0$ or $1$.
\end{proof}
